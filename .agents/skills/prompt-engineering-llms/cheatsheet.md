# Cheatsheet

### Cheatsheet: Decision Tables, Comparison Matrices, and Quick Reference Rules  

#### **Decision Tables**  
- **What They Are**: A tabular representation of all possible conditions and their corresponding actions or outcomes.  
- **When to Use**: To systematically evaluate multiple conditions and determine the optimal action for each combination.  
- **Structure**:  
  | **Condition 1** | **Condition 2** | **Action/Outcome** |  
  |----------------|----------------|--------------------|  
  | Condition 1a    | Condition 2b    | Action 1           |  
  | Condition 1c    | Condition 2d    | Outcome 2          |  
- **How Many Conditions?**: Typically up to 3–4 conditions per table for clarity.  
- **Symbols/Abbreviations**: Use shorthand for brevity (e.g., "High" instead of "High Performance").  
- **Example Scenarios**:  
  - Choosing a car: Evaluate price, fuel efficiency, and features.  
  - Selecting a hotel: Compare location, cost, and amenities.  
- **Best Practices**:  
  - Keep conditions mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive.  
  - Use simple actions or outcomes to avoid complexity.  
- **Limitations**: Decision tables can become unwieldy with too many conditions or complex interactions.  

---

#### **Comparison Matrices**  
- **What They Are**: A matrix that compares attributes of options side by side, facilitating pairwise evaluation.  
- **When to Use**: To compare and contrast multiple items based on predefined criteria.  
- **Types**:  
  - **Pros-and-Con List**: Simple list of pros and cons for each option.  
  - **Comparison Matrix**: Grid format with rows representing options and columns representing criteria.  
- **Structure**:  
  | **Criteria** | **Option A** | **Option B** | **Option C** |  
  |--------------|-------------|-------------|-------------|  
  | Criterion 1  | Yes         | No          | Maybe       |  
  | Criterion 2  | High        | Medium      | Low         |  
- **Example Scenarios**:  
  - Choosing a laptop: Compare price, performance, and features.  
  - Comparing investment options: Evaluate risk, return, and volatility.  
- **Best Practices**:  
  - Define criteria in advance to ensure consistency.  
  - Use numerical ratings for clarity (e.g., scale from 1–5).  
- **Limitations**: Comparison matrices can be time-consuming to develop if criteria are unclear or too granular.  

---

#### **Quick Reference Rules**  
- **What They Are**: Simple, actionable guidelines or shortcuts for making decisions quickly and efficiently.  
- **When to Use**: When you need a fast decision-making process without full analysis.  
- **Structure**: If-Then statements that encapsulate key criteria.  
  - Example: "If the budget is tight AND features are limited, then choose the most reliable option."  
- **Example Scenarios**:  
  - Hiring decisions: "If experience is lacking AND performance is average, then interview shortlisted candidates."  
  - Selecting a college: "If location is critical AND budget is moderate, then prioritize public universities."  
- **Best Practices**:  
  - Keep rules simple and focused on the most critical criteria.  
  - Test rules with real-world scenarios to ensure effectiveness.  
- **Limitations**: Quick reference rules may oversimplify complex decisions or ignore nuances.  

---

### Conclusion  
Decision tables, comparison matrices, and quick reference rules are powerful tools for structured decision-making. By applying these frameworks, practitioners can make informed choices more efficiently and systematically.
